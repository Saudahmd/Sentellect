Key Concepts

Essential Circle Properties

Normal to a Circle:
The normal at any point on a circle passes through the center of the circle.
Tangent and Radius:
The radius drawn to the point of tangency is perpendicular to the tangent line.
Right Triangle Circumcenter:
The midpoint of the hypotenuse of a right triangle is the circumcenter of the triangle.
Geometric Mean Property:
The perpendicular from a point on a circle to its diameter is the geometric mean between the segments of the diameter.
Conic Sections Definition:
A conic section is the set of all points P such that PF/PM = e (a constant), where F is a focus and PM is the distance to a directrix.

Important Geometric Relationships:
1. Normal line passes through center of circle
2. Tangent is perpendicular to radius at point of contact
3. Right triangle's circumcenter is at midpoint of hypotenuse
4. Perpendicular from point on circle to diameter is geometric mean
Problem 1: Prove that normal lines of a circle pass through the centre of the circle.
Step 1: Consider a circle with center O and radius r.
Step 2: Let P be any point on the circle, so OP = r.
Step 3: The tangent at P is perpendicular to the radius OP.
Step 4: The normal at P is perpendicular to the tangent at P.
Step 5: Since both the radius and normal are perpendicular to the tangent at P, they must be collinear.
Step 6: Therefore, the normal at P passes through the center O.
Problem 2: Prove that the straight line drawn from the centre of a circle perpendicular to a tangent passes through the point of tangency.
Step 1: Consider a circle with center O and a tangent line l.
Step 2: Draw a line from O perpendicular to l, meeting l at point P.
Step 3: We need to prove that P is the point of tangency.
Step 4: Suppose there's another point Q on l that lies on the circle.
Step 5: Then OQ would be a radius, and triangle OPQ would be a right triangle with right angle at P.
Step 6: In this triangle, OQ would be the hypotenuse, so OQ > OP.
Step 7: But if Q is on the circle, OQ = r, and OP < r, which contradicts the definition of a tangent (touching at exactly one point).
Step 8: Therefore, P must be the unique point of tangency.
Problem 3: Prove that the mid point of the hypotenuse of a right triangle is the circumcentre of the triangle.
Step 1: Consider a right triangle ABC with right angle at C.
Step 2: Let M be the midpoint of the hypotenuse AB.
Step 3: Draw line MC. We need to show that MA = MB = MC.
Step 4: Since M is the midpoint of AB, we have MA = MB.
Step 5: Draw a rectangle by constructing point D such that ABCD is a rectangle.
Step 6: In a rectangle, diagonals bisect each other, so M is also the midpoint of CD.
Step 7: Therefore, MC = MD = MA = MB.
Step 8: This means M is equidistant from all three vertices, so it is the circumcenter.
Problem 4: Prove that the perpendicular dropped from a point of a circle on a diameter is a mean proportional between the segments into which it divides the diameter.
Step 1: Consider a circle with diameter AB.
Step 2: Let P be any point on the circle, and drop a perpendicular PC to AB.
Step 3: This divides AB into segments AC and CB.
Step 4: We need to prove that PC is the geometric mean of AC and CB, i.e., PC¬≤ = AC √ó CB.
Step 5: Since AB is a diameter, angle APB is a right angle (angle in a semicircle).
Step 6: In right triangle APB, PC is the altitude to the hypotenuse.
Step 7: In a right triangle, the altitude to the hypotenuse is the geometric mean between the segments of the hypotenuse.
Step 8: Therefore, PC¬≤ = AC √ó CB, which proves the statement.

Conic Sections

Introduction to Conic Sections

Learn about parabola, ellipse, and hyperbola - the curves formed by intersecting a plane with a cone.

üìê
Definition
Definition: A conic section is the set of all points P such that PF/PM = e, where F is a focus and PM is the distance to a directrix.
‚é∑
Parabola
Property: When e = 1, the conic is a parabola - all points equidistant from focus and directrix.
‚óã
Ellipse
Property: When 0 < e < 1, the conic is an ellipse - sum of distances to two foci is constant.
‚àû
Hyperbola
Property: When e > 1, the conic is a hyperbola - difference of distances to two foci is constant.
Conic Sections Classification:
‚Ä¢ Parabola (e = 1): Set of points equidistant from a focus and a directrix
‚Ä¢ Ellipse (0 < e < 1): Set of points where sum of distances to two foci is constant
‚Ä¢ Hyperbola (e > 1): Set of points where difference of distances to two foci is constant
‚Ä¢ Circle (special case of ellipse with e = 0): Set of points equidistant from a center
Understanding the Definition: PF/PM = e
Step 1: Let F be a fixed point (focus) and L be a fixed line (directrix) in a plane.
Step 2: For any point P in the plane, let PF be the distance from P to F.
Step 3: Let PM be the perpendicular distance from P to the line L.
Step 4: The set of all points P such that PF/PM = e (a constant) is called a conic section.
Step 5: The constant e is called the eccentricity of the conic.
Step 6: When e = 1, we get a parabola (PF = PM).
Step 7: When 0 < e < 1, we get an ellipse (PF < PM).
Step 8: When e > 1, we get a hyperbola (PF > PM).
Visualizing Different Conic Sections
Step 1: Imagine a double-napped cone (two cones vertex to vertex).
Step 2: When a plane cuts parallel to the side of the cone, we get a parabola.
Step 3: When a plane cuts at an angle less steep than the side, we get an ellipse.
Step 4: When a plane cuts steeper than the side, we get a hyperbola.
Step 5: A circle is a special case of an ellipse when the cutting plane is perpendicular to the axis.
Step 6: These curves are called "conic sections" because they are formed by sectioning a cone.
