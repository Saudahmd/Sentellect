Point of Intersection of Two Lines

Where Do Lines Meet?

Explore how to find the point where two lines intersect using simultaneous equations.

Understanding Line Intersection

What is Line Intersection?

When two non-parallel lines cross each other on a coordinate plane, they intersect at exactly one point. This point satisfies the equations of both lines.

How to Find Intersection?

We solve the system of equations formed by both lines using methods like substitution, elimination, or cross-multiplication.

The cross-multiplication formula is:

\( \frac{x}{b_1c_2 - b_2c_1} = \frac{y}{c_1a_2 - c_2a_1} = \frac{1}{a_1b_2 - a_2b_1} \)

Visual Representation

Imagine two roads crossing at an intersection. The intersection point is where both roads meet. In mathematics, we can precisely calculate the coordinates of this meeting point.

This concept is used in navigation, computer graphics, and optimization problems.

‚Ä¢ Two non-parallel lines always intersect at exactly one point

‚Ä¢ Parallel lines never intersect (they have the same slope)

‚Ä¢ Coincident lines have infinite intersection points

‚Ä¢ The intersection point satisfies both line equations

Example 1: Simple Intersection

Find the point of intersection of x-2y+1=0 and 2x-y+2=0

Step 1: Identify coefficients: a‚ÇÅ=1, b‚ÇÅ=-2, c‚ÇÅ=1; a‚ÇÇ=2, b‚ÇÇ=-1, c‚ÇÇ=2
Step 2: Calculate denominator: D = a‚ÇÅb‚ÇÇ - a‚ÇÇb‚ÇÅ = (1)(-1) - (2)(-2) = -1 + 4 = 3
Step 3: Calculate x: x = (b‚ÇÅc‚ÇÇ - b‚ÇÇc‚ÇÅ)/D = ((-2)(2) - (-1)(1))/3 = (-4 + 1)/3 = -1
Step 4: Calculate y: y = (c‚ÇÅa‚ÇÇ - c‚ÇÇa‚ÇÅ)/D = ((1)(2) - (2)(1))/3 = (2 - 2)/3 = 0
Step 5: Interpretation: The lines intersect at (-1, 0)

Example 3: Line Through Intersection

Find the line through intersection of 2x+5y-8=0 and 3x-4y-6=0 that passes through (2,1)

Concurrency of Three Lines

When Three Lines Meet at One Point

Learn how to determine if three lines are concurrent (meet at a single point).

Understanding Concurrency

What is Concurrency?

Three lines are concurrent if they all pass through the same point. This means there's a single point that satisfies all three line equations simultaneously.

How to Check Concurrency?

We can check concurrency by:

1. Finding the intersection of two lines and checking if it satisfies the third line

2. Using the determinant method: \( \begin{vmatrix} a_1 & b_1 & c_1 \\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 \\ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \end{vmatrix} = 0 \)

Visual Representation

Imagine three roads meeting at a central square. If all three roads pass through the same square, they're concurrent. If one road misses the square, they're not concurrent.

This concept is used in geometry, computer graphics, and navigation.

Key Properties

‚Ä¢ Three lines are concurrent if they meet at a single point

‚Ä¢ The determinant of their coefficients must be zero

‚Ä¢ Any two lines always intersect (unless parallel)

‚Ä¢ Three lines may or may not be concurrent

Concurrency Test: Three lines are concurrent if \( \begin{vmatrix} a_1 & b_1 & c_1 \\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 \\ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \end{vmatrix} = 0 \)

üí° Memory Tip: If the determinant is zero, all three lines meet at one point!

The Magician's Trick

The Magician draws three paths. If they all meet, the magic works (Determinant=0). If they miss each other, the trick fails! We solve for the concurrency point by finding the intersection of the first two lines, and checking if the third line passes through it.

üìç
Example 1: Finding Unknown Parameter

Find p if 2x+3y-1=0, 3x-y-5=0, and 3x+py+8=0 are concurrent

Step 1: Set up determinant: \( \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 3 & -1 \\ 3 & -1 & -5 \\ 3 & p & 8 \end{vmatrix} = 0 \)
Step 2: Expand along first row: 2((-1)(8) - (-5)(p)) - 3((3)(8) - (-5)(3)) + (-1)((3)(p) - (-1)(3)) = 0
Step 3: Simplify: 2(-8 + 5p) - 3(24 + 15) - 1(3p + 3) = 0
Step 4: Combine: -16 + 10p - 117 - 3p - 3 = 0
Step 5: Solve: 7p - 136 = 0 ‚áí p = 136/7
Step 6: Interpretation: The lines are concurrent when p = 136/7
üó∫Ô∏è
Example 2: Verifying Concurrency

Show that 4x-3y-8=0, 3x-4y-6=0, and x-y-2=0 are concurrent and find the point

Step 1: Check determinant: \( \begin{vmatrix} 4 & -3 & -8 \\ 3 & -4 & -6 \\ 1 & -1 & -2 \end{vmatrix} \)
Step 2: Expand: 4((-4)(-2) - (-6)(-1)) - (-3)((3)(-2) - (-6)(1)) + (-8)((3)(-1) - (-4)(1))
Step 3: Calculate: 4(8-6) + 3(-6+6) - 8(-3+4) = 4(2) + 3(0) - 8(1) = 8 + 0 - 8 = 0
Step 4: Since determinant is 0, lines are concurrent
Step 5: Find intersection of first two lines: 4x-3y=8 and 3x-4y=6
Step 6: Solve: Multiply first by 4, second by 3: 16x-12y=32 and 9x-12y=18
Step 7: Subtract: 7x=14 ‚áí x=2, then y=0
Step 8: Interpretation: The concurrency point is (2, 0)
üßÆ
Example 3: Non-Concurrent Lines

Check concurrency of 2x+3y+4=0, x-2y-3=0, and 3x+y-8=0

Step 1: Set up determinant: \( \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 1 & -2 & -3 \\ 3 & 1 & -8 \end{vmatrix} \)
Step 2: Expand along first row: 2((-2)(-8) - (-3)(1)) - 3((1)(-8) - (-3)(3)) + 4((1)(1) - (-2)(3))
Step 3: Calculate: 2(16+3) - 3(-8+9) + 4(1+6) = 2(19) - 3(1) + 4(7)
Step 4: Simplify: 38 - 3 + 28 = 63
Step 5: Since determinant is 63 ‚â† 0, lines are not concurrent
Step 6: Interpretation: The three lines form a triangle, not meeting at a single point

Angle Between Two Lines

Measuring the Angle Between Lines

Learn how to calculate the angle between two lines using their slopes.

Understanding Angle Between Lines

What is Angle Between Lines?

The angle between two lines is the smaller angle formed when they intersect. It's measured in degrees or radians and can be calculated using their slopes.

How to Calculate Angle?

For lines with slopes m‚ÇÅ and m‚ÇÇ, the angle Œ∏ between them is:

\( \tan\theta = \left| \frac{m_2 - m_1}{1 + m_1m_2} \right| \)

Special cases:

‚Ä¢ If m‚ÇÅm‚ÇÇ = -1, lines are perpendicular (Œ∏ = 90¬∞)

‚Ä¢ If m‚ÇÅ = m‚ÇÇ, lines are parallel (Œ∏ = 0¬∞)

Visual Representation

Imagine two roads crossing at an intersection. The angle between them tells us how sharp the turn is. A small angle means a sharp turn, while a large angle means a gentle turn.

This concept is used in engineering, navigation, and computer graphics.

‚Ä¢ The angle is always between 0¬∞ and 90¬∞

‚Ä¢ Parallel lines have angle 0¬∞

‚Ä¢ The formula gives the acute angle between lines

Angle Formula: \( \tan\theta = \left| \frac{m_2 - m_1}{1 + m_1m_2} \right| \)

üí° Memory Tip: The formula uses the difference of slopes divided by 1 plus their product!

A builder is checking if two walls meet at a right angle (90¬∞). If the product of their slopes (m‚ÇÅm‚ÇÇ) is -1, they are perfect! If not, they use the tan(Œ∏) formula to measure the error angle.

Find the acute angle between 2x+3y=0 and x-y+1=0

Step 2: Apply formula: \( \tan\theta = \left| \frac{1 - (-2/3)}{1 + (1)(-2/3)} \right| \)
Step 3: Simplify: \( \tan\theta = \left| \frac{5/3}{1/3} \right| = 5 \)

Special Triangle Centers

Finding Centers in Triangles

Learn about the special points in triangles: centroid, orthocenter, and circumcenter.

Understanding Triangle Centers

What are Triangle Centers?

Triangle centers are special points in a triangle that have unique geometric properties. The most important ones are centroid, orthocenter, and circumcenter.

Types of Triangle Centers

‚Ä¢ Centroid (G): Intersection of medians (lines from vertices to midpoints of opposite sides)

‚Ä¢ Orthocenter (H): Intersection of altitudes (lines from vertices perpendicular to opposite sides)

‚Ä¢ Circumcenter (O): Intersection of perpendicular bisectors of sides

Visual Representation

Imagine a triangle as a physical object. The centroid is its center of mass (balance point), the orthocenter relates to its heights, and the circumcenter is the center of the circle that passes through all three vertices.

These concepts are used in engineering, architecture, and computer graphics.

Key Properties

‚Ä¢ In an equilateral triangle, all centers coincide

‚Ä¢ In a right triangle, the circumcenter is the midpoint of the hypotenuse

‚Ä¢ The centroid divides each median in a 2:1 ratio

‚Ä¢ The orthocenter, centroid, and circumcenter are collinear (Euler line)

The Engineer's Challenge

An engineer must calculate the Orthocenter to understand the internal forces (heights) and the Circumcenter to plan for the circular foundation (circumcircle). These calculations are complex but follow a strict, logical sequence!

üìç
Example 1: Finding the Orthocenter

Find the orthocenter of triangle with vertices A(-2,3), B(-4,1), C(3,5)

Step 1 (Altitude 1): Slope of AB: m_AB = 1. Perpendicular slope is m_perp = -1
Step 2: Altitude on AB passes through C(3,5): y-5 = -1(x-3) ‚áí x+y-8=0 (L‚ÇÅ)
Step 3 (Altitude 2): Slope of BC: m_BC = 4/7. Perpendicular slope is m_perp = -7/4
Step 4: Altitude on BC passes through A(-2,3): y-3 = -7/4(x+2) ‚áí 7x+4y+2=0 (L‚ÇÇ)
Step 5: Solve L‚ÇÅ and L‚ÇÇ: From L‚ÇÅ, y=8-x. Substitute into L‚ÇÇ: 7x+4(8-x)+2=0 ‚áí 3x+34=0
Step 6: x = -34/3. Then y = 8 - (-34/3) = 58/3
Step 7: Interpretation: The orthocenter is (-34/3, 58/3)
üó∫Ô∏è
Example 2: Finding the Circumcenter

Find the circumcenter of triangle with vertices A(-2,3), B(-4,1), C(3,5)

Step 1 (Bisector 1 - AB): Midpoint D of AB: (-3, 2). Perpendicular slope is m_perp = -1
Step 2: Bisector on AB passes through D(-3,2): y-2 = -1(x+3) ‚áí x+y+1=0 (L‚ÇÉ)
Step 3 (Bisector 2 - BC): Midpoint E of BC: (-1/2, 3). Perpendicular slope is m_perp = -7/4
Step 4: Bisector on BC passes through E(-1/2, 3): y-3 = -7/4(x+1/2) ‚áí 14x+8y-17=0 (L‚ÇÑ)
Step 5: Solve L‚ÇÉ and L‚ÇÑ: Substitute y=-1-x into L‚ÇÑ: 14x+8(-1-x)-17=0
Step 6: 6x-25=0 ‚áí x = 25/6. Then y = -1 - (25/6) = -31/6
Step 7: Interpretation: The circumcenter is (25/6, -31/6)

Example 3: Area of Triangle

Find the area of triangle formed by lines 7x-y-10=0, 10x+y-41=0, and 3x+2y+3=0

Step 1: Find intersection of L1 and L2: (3, 11) (Vertex A)
Step 2: Find intersection of L2 and L3: (5, -9) (Vertex B)
Step 3: Find intersection of L1 and L3: (1, -3) (Vertex C)
Step 4: Use area formula: Area = 1/2 √ó |x‚ÇÅ(y‚ÇÇ-y‚ÇÉ) + x‚ÇÇ(y‚ÇÉ-y‚ÇÅ) + x‚ÇÉ(y‚ÇÅ-y‚ÇÇ)|
Step 5: Substitute: Area = 1/2 √ó |3(-9+3) + 5(-3-11) + 1(11+9)|
Step 6: Calculate: 1/2 √ó |3(-6) + 5(-14) + 1(20)| = 1/2 √ó |-18 -70 + 20| = 1/2 √ó |-68|
Step 7: Interpretation: Area = 34 square units
