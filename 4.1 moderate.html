Distance Formula

Distance Between Two Points

Find how far apart two points are using the distance formula.

üö∂‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è
Walking Distance
Easy: If you know coordinates of two places, the distance formula tells you the straight-line distance between them.
üìê
Pythagorean Connection
Easy: The distance formula comes from the Pythagorean theorem - the distance is the hypotenuse of a right triangle.
üó∫Ô∏è
Map Distance
Easy: Just like measuring straight-line distance on a map between two cities using their coordinates.
Distance Formula: \( d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \)
Example 1: A(3,1) and B(-2,-4)
Step 1: \( d = \sqrt{(-2-3)^2 + (-4-1)^2} \)
Step 2: \( = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + (-5)^2} = \sqrt{25 + 25} \)
Step 3: \( = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2} \)
Example 2: A(-8,3) and B(2,-1)
Step 1: \( d = \sqrt{(2+8)^2 + (-1-3)^2} \)
Step 2: \( = \sqrt{(10)^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{100 + 16} \)
Step 3: \( = \sqrt{116} = 2\sqrt{29} \)
Example 3: A(-‚àö5, -1/3) and B(-3‚àö5, 5)
Step 1: \( d = \sqrt{(-3\sqrt{5} + \sqrt{5})^2 + (5 + \frac{1}{3})^2} \)
Step 2: \( = \sqrt{(-2\sqrt{5})^2 + (\frac{16}{3})^2} \)
Step 3: \( = \sqrt{20 + \frac{256}{9}} = \sqrt{\frac{436}{9}} \)
Step 4: \( = \frac{2\sqrt{109}}{3} \)

Midpoint Formula

Finding the Midpoint

Find the point exactly halfway between two given points.

ü§ù
Meeting Point
Easy: If two friends want to meet halfway between their houses, the midpoint formula finds that exact location.
‚öñÔ∏è
Center Point
Easy: The midpoint is like the center of a line segment - perfectly balanced between the endpoints.
üåâ
Bridge Center
Easy: The midpoint of a bridge is exactly halfway between its two ends - that's what we calculate!
Midpoint Formula: \( M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right) \)
Example 1: A(3,1) and B(-2,-4)
Step 1: \( M = \left( \frac{3 + (-2)}{2}, \frac{1 + (-4)}{2} \right) \)
Step 2: \( = \left( \frac{1}{2}, -\frac{3}{2} \right) \)
Example 2: A(-8,3) and B(2,-1)
Step 1: \( M = \left( \frac{-8 + 2}{2}, \frac{3 + (-1)}{2} \right) \)
Step 2: \( = \left( \frac{-6}{2}, \frac{2}{2} \right) = (-3, 1) \)
Example 3: A(-‚àö5, -1/3) and B(-3‚àö5, 5)
Step 1: \( M = \left( \frac{-\sqrt{5} - 3\sqrt{5}}{2}, \frac{-\frac{1}{3} + 5}{2} \right) \)
Step 2: \( = \left( \frac{-4\sqrt{5}}{2}, \frac{\frac{14}{3}}{2} \right) \)
Step 3: \( = \left( -2\sqrt{5}, \frac{7}{3} \right) \)

Section Formula

Dividing a Line Segment

Find a point that divides a line segment in a given ratio.

üç´
Sharing Chocolate
Easy: If you want to divide a chocolate bar between friends in a specific ratio, the section formula finds where to break it.
üåâ
Bridge Placement
Easy: If you need to place a bridge closer to one side of a river than the other, the section formula finds that spot.
üèÅ
Race Track
Easy: On a race track, if you want to mark a point that's 2/3 of the way from start to finish, use section formula!
Section Formula: \( P = \left( \frac{mx_2 + nx_1}{m+n}, \frac{my_2 + ny_1}{m+n} \right) \)
Where m:n is the ratio in which point divides the segment
Example 1: A(-1,4) and B(6,2) in ratio 1:2
Step 1: \( P = \left( \frac{1(6) + 2(-1)}{1+2}, \frac{1(2) + 2(4)}{1+2} \right) \)
Step 2: \( = \left( \frac{6 - 2}{3}, \frac{2 + 8}{3} \right) \)
Step 3: \( = \left( \frac{4}{3}, \frac{10}{3} \right) \)
Example 2: A(-5,8) and B(5,3) in ratio 3:2
Step 1: \( P = \left( \frac{3(5) + 2(-5)}{3+2}, \frac{3(3) + 2(8)}{3+2} \right) \)
Step 2: \( = \left( \frac{15 - 10}{5}, \frac{9 + 16}{5} \right) \)
Step 3: \( = \left( 1, 5 \right) \)
Example 3: A(1,4) and B(5,6) in ratio 2:1
Step 1: \( P = \left( \frac{2(5) + 1(1)}{2+1}, \frac{2(6) + 1(4)}{2+1} \right) \)
Step 2: \( = \left( \frac{10 + 1}{3}, \frac{12 + 4}{3} \right) \)
Step 3: \( = \left( \frac{11}{3}, \frac{16}{3} \right) \)

Collinearity of Points

Checking if Points are Collinear

Determine if three points lie on the same straight line.

üìø
Beads on String
Easy: Collinear points are like beads on a straight string - they all line up perfectly.
üöß
Fence Posts
Easy: If fence posts are in a straight line, they're collinear - just like our points!
üöÇ
Train Tracks
Easy: Points along straight train tracks are collinear - they follow the same path.
Method 1 (Slopes): Points are collinear if slopes between all pairs are equal.
Method 2 (Area): Points are collinear if area of triangle formed by them is zero.
Example 1: A(-1,1), B(3,2), C(7,3)
Step 1: Find slope AB = \( \frac{2-1}{3-(-1)} = \frac{1}{4} \)
Step 2: Find slope BC = \( \frac{3-2}{7-3} = \frac{1}{4} \)
Step 3: Since slopes are equal, points are collinear
Example 2: A(1,2), B(2,4), C(3,6)
Step 1: Find slope AB = \( \frac{4-2}{2-1} = 2 \)
Step 2: Find slope BC = \( \frac{6-4}{3-2} = 2 \)
Step 3: Since slopes are equal, points are collinear
Example 3: A(0,0), B(2,2), C(2,0)
Step 1: Find slope AB = \( \frac{2-0}{2-0} = 1 \)
Step 2: Find slope BC = \( \frac{0-2}{2-2} = \frac{-2}{0} \) (undefined)
Step 3: Since slopes are different, points are not collinear
