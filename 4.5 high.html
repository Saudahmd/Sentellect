1. Homogeneous Equation & Lines

The Origin Pair: ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = 0

This second-degree equation always represents a **pair of straight lines passing through the origin** (0,0).

üè° Homogeneous = Always Home

Think of the origin (0,0) as the **home** of the lines. Since the equation ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = 0 **has no constant term**, it means that if you plug in (x, y) = (0, 0), the equation holds true (0=0).

Analogy: Imagine two crossing roads. The intersection point is **always** the origin (0,0). The homogeneous equation is the map showing just these two roads passing through that center point.

Key Insight: The process of **factoring** the quadratic is what separates the two lines: ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = (m1x - y)(m2x - y) = 0. Each factor represents one straight line passing through the origin.

Example 1: Factoring and Finding Lines

Find the lines represented by 10x2 - 23xy - 5y2 = 0.

Step 1: Factor middle term: 10x2 - 25xy + 2xy - 5y2 = 0.
Step 2: Group and factor: 5x(2x - 5y) + y(2x - 5y) = 0.
Step 3: Joint equation: (2x - 5y)(5x + y) = 0.
Answer: The two separate lines are 2x - 5y = 0 and 5x + y = 0.
Example 2: Factoring with General Coefficients

Find the lines represented by 2x2 + 3xy - 5y2 = 0.

Step 1: Factor middle term: 2x2 + 5xy - 2xy - 5y2 = 0.
Step 2: Group and factor: x(2x + 5y) - y(2x + 5y) = 0.
Step 3: Joint equation: (2x + 5y)(x - y) = 0.
Answer: The two separate lines are 2x + 5y = 0 and x - y = 0.
Example 3: Coincident Lines (Repeated Roots)

Find the lines represented by 9x2 + 24xy + 16y2 = 0.

Step 1: Recognize perfect square: (3x)2 + 2(3x)(4y) + (4y)2 = 0.
Step 2: Factor: (3x + 4y)2 = 0.
Answer:The equation represents two coincident lines: 3x + 4y = 0. (Angle is 0circ).

2. Angle Between the Lines

Angle Formula: tanŒ∏ = | 2 ‚àö{h2- ab}a + b |

The **acute angle** (Œ∏) between the two lines can be found directly from the coefficients **a, h, b** of the equation.

üìè Angle Detector

Instead of factoring the equation to find two separate lines, calculating their slopes (m1, m2), and *then* using the slope formula (tan Œ∏ = |(m1 - m2) / (1 + m1 m2)|), we can use this **shortcut!**

Analogy: Think of this formula as a smart protractor. You just input the three magic numbers (a, 2h, b) from the equation, and it instantly spits out the angle, saving you all the factoring work!

Key Conditions:

  • If h2 - ab > 0, the lines are **real and distinct**.
  • If a + b = 0, the lines are **perpendicular** (Œ∏ = 90circ).
  • If h2- ab = 0, the lines are **coincident** (Œ∏ = 0circ).

Example 1: Finding Acute Angle

Find Œ∏ for 10x2 - 23xy - 5y2 = 0. (a=10, b=-5, h=-23/2)

Step 1: Identify: a=10, b=-5, h = -23{2}.
Step 2: Calculate h2- ab = /{5294 + 50} = /{7294} = 272.
Step 3: Substitute: tanŒ∏ = | 2 ¬∑ /{272}10 + (-5) | = 275.
Answer:Œ∏ = arctan(275) approx 79circ 31'.
Example 2: Obtuse and Acute Angles

Find the angle Œ∏ between 2x2 + 3xy - 5y2 = 0. (a=2, b=-5, h=3/2)

Step 1: Identify: a=2, b=-5, h = 3{2}.
Step 2: Substitute: tanŒ∏ = | 2 ‚àö{/{94 + 10}}2 - 5 | = | 2 ¬∑ /{72}-3 | = | -73 |.
Step 3: Œ∏_acute = arctan(73) approx 66circ 48'.
Answer:The acute angle is 66circ 48'. The obtuse angle is 180circ - 66circ 48' = 113circ 12'.

Find Œ∏ for x2- 2xy secalpha + y2= 0. (Use identity sec2alpha - 1 = tan2alpha)

Step 1: Identify: a=1, b=1, h=-secalpha.
Step 2: Substitute: tanŒ∏ = | 2 ‚àö{(-secalpha)2- (1)(1)}1 + 1 |.
Step 3: Simplify: tanŒ∏ = 2 ‚àö{sec2alpha - 1}2 = tan2alpha = |tanalpha|.

3. Perpendicular Joint Equation

The Perpendicular Flip: bx2 - 2hxy + ay2 = 0

The joint equation of lines perpendicular to ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = 0 is a simple **swap** of a and b and a **sign change** of the 2h term.

üîÑ The Coefficient Swap Trick

If you have a line m x - y = 0, the perpendicular line is -x - m y = 0, or x + m y = 0. The coefficients of x and y are swapped and one is negated. The same idea applies to the joint equation!

The Trick: To find the equation perpendicular to ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = 0:

  • The coefficient of x2 becomes b.
  • The coefficient of y2 becomes a.
  • The sign of the xy term (2h) is flipped.

Quick Check: The sum of x2 and y2 coefficients in the original equation is a+b. In the perpendicular equation, the sum is b+a. The sums are the same! (This is useful, but only a+b=0 guarantees perpendicular lines).

Origin (0,0)
90¬∞
Example 1: General Perpendicular Form

Find the joint equation perpendicular to ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = 0.

Step 1: Substitute x \rightarrow y and y \rightarrow -x into the original equation.
Step 2: a(y)2 + 2h(y)(-x) + b(-x)2 = 0.
Step 3: ay2 - 2hxy + bx2 = 0.
Answer: bx2 - 2hxy + ay2 = 0. (This confirms the swap rule).
Example 2: Simple Perpendicular Form

Find the joint equation perpendicular to 10x2 - 23xy - 5y2 = 0.

Step 1: Original: a=10, b=-5, 2h=-23.
Step 2: Apply the swap: b \rightarrow -5, a \rightarrow 10.
Step 3: Change xy sign: -23xy \rightarrow +23xy.
Step 4: Construct: -5x2 + 23xy + 10y2 = 0.
Answer: 5x2 - 23xy - 10y2 = 0. (Multiply by -1 for positive x2 term).
Example 3: Perpendicular to x2 - 2xy \tan\alpha - y2 = 0

Since a+b=0 (1 + (-1) = 0), the original lines are already perpendicular.

Step 1: Original: a=1, b=-1, 2h=-2\tan\alpha.
Step 2: Apply the swap: b \rightarrow -1, a \rightarrow 1.
Step 3: Change xy sign: -2xy \tan\alpha \rightarrow +2xy \tan\alpha.
Step 4: Construct: -x2 + 2xy \tan\alpha + y2 = 0.
Answer: x2 - 2xy \tan\alpha - y2 = 0 (The original equation is the same as the perpendicular one).

4. Area Bounded by Lines

Area Bounded by L1, L2 and L3

To find the area of the triangle formed by the homogeneous pair (L1, L2) and a third line (L3), you need the three vertices.

üî∫ The Triangle Locator

A homogeneous equation ax2 + 2hxy + by2 = 0 is two lines (L1 and L2) that always intersect at the **origin**, which is your first vertex A(0,0).

Finding Vertices:

  • A: Intersection of L1 and L2 (always **(0,0)**).
  • B: Intersection of L1 and L3.
  • C: Intersection of L2 and L3.

You must first **factor** the homogeneous equation to get L1 and L2.

Area Shortcut (with Origin): Since one vertex is A(0,0), the standard area formula simplifies significantly for vertices (xA, yA)=(0,0), B(xB, yB), and C(xC, yC):

Area = 1{2} | xB yC - xC yB |

Example: Area bounded by 10x2 - xy - 21y2 = 0 and x + y + 1 = 0

Factoring gives: L1: 2x - 3y = 0 and L2: 5x + 7y = 0. L3: x + y + 1 = 0.

Step 1: Vertices: A(0, 0).
Step 2: L1 \cap L3: Solve 2x - 3y = 0 and x = -y - 1 to get B(-3{5}, -2{5}).
Step 3: L2 \cap L3: Solve 5x + 7y = 0 and x = -y - 1 to get C(7{2}, -9{2}).
Step 4: Use Area formula (simplified for A(0,0)): 1{2} | xB yC - xC yB |.
Step 5: Area = 1{2} \left| (-3{5})(-9{2}) - (7{2})(-2{5}) \right| = 1{2} \left| 27{10} + 14{10} \right|.
Answer: Area is 41{20} sq. units (or 2.05 sq. units).
